Art

Newcastle is full of Art, there is more art on the streets than in Art Galleries and Museums. Much of the art is unnoticed, and nearly all of it unexplained, a caption on a base plate if you are lucky. Over the years there have been pamplets produced to show where this 'Street Art' is and a brief description of what the art is about. These pamphlets tend to become out of date very quickly and rarely get updated. Newcastle and Gateshead's Riverside have a lot of art, some a hangover from Gateshead's Garden Festival, other more monumental pieces, part of the regeneration of the Quayside. Ouseburn is also well worth a visit.

Attractions

Like many European cities, Newcastle has hundreds of years of history and has a wide range of architecture. Many of Newcastle's attractions are not hidden at all, this does not make them obvious. Sometimes all is needed is to 'Look Up' or 'Look Down', take a tentative step inside a building, or stand in a particular place. In a busy city like Newcastle it is sometimes difficult to stop and look, either because of fast moving cars or in crowds of pedestrians. It feels unnatural to remain in one place and look, even though there are plenty of things to see. Hopefully visitors to this site will find things they've never noticed before.

Heritage

Many aspects of Newcastle's heritage have, at one time or another, been captured in public information panels. Jesmond Dene, Exhibition Park as well as Newcastle City Centre have an abundance of such panels. Other areas of the city are less well endowed. Newcastle's Riverside to the west of the Redheugh Bridge, once a powerhouse of industry and ship building, has some very good recently restored panels. Unfortunately many of heritage panels throughout the city have faded over time making them impossible to read, others have been defaced by vandals or are have vanished from their original location.

Parks

One of the unique things about Newcastle is the sheer size and number of its parks, compared to other UK cities. The Town Moor is larger than Hampstead Heath and all the Royal London Parks combined. There is a splendid array of Victorian Parks; Jesmond Dene, Heaton and Armstrong Parks as well as Exhibition Park, all much used and loved by local residents and visitors.

Plaques

Commemorative 'blue' plaques, started in London, and then spread to regional cities. Amongst the more interesting plaques in Newcastle are those which indicate the homes of notable former residents. Many locals could not tell you where Robert Stephenson, Mo Mowlem, Basil Hume or Wittgenstein lived, yet their former homes bear plaques to this effect.
